# I have a meeting with a lender tomorrow and I need to be sure of my numbers and also come up with a cheat sheet or talking points

You can walk in with a tight, simple story: 3-year numbers that you can say from memory, plus 6–8 talking points that link those numbers to how you’ll repay the loan.[^1][^2]

## Core numbers to memorize

Use these as your **cheat** sheet headline:

- Loan amount: 6,500 SBA microloan (equipment, cloud, insurance, LLC, initial marketing).[^3]
- Year 1 revenue: 90,000.[^1]
- Year 2 revenue: 135,000.[^1]
- Year 3 revenue: 180,000.[^1]
- Year 1 net profit after all expenses and interest: 80,749.[^1]
- Monthly loan payment (if they ask): say “about 100–200 per month depending on final term and rate; my projections leave a lot of room above that.” (You can keep this conceptual and let them fill in exact amortization.)

Simple project math you can say out loud:

- “I get to 90,000 in Year 1 with roughly 15–18 projects at 5,000–6,000 each, which matches the pricing in my plan.”[^3][^1]


## One-page talking points (print this)

You can literally print this section and have it in front of you.

1. Who you are and what Silva Automation does
    - “I’m Savaiinaea ‘Naea’ Silva, founder of Silva Automation, a workflow automation consulting business in Waipahu.”[^3]
    - “I build fixed-price automation projects for Hawaii service businesses that are tired of manual admin work in tools they already use.”[^3]
2. Market and opportunity
    - “Hawaii has about 144,000 small businesses; almost half the workforce is in small firms, and many of them still do admin by hand.”[^3]
    - “I’m focused on established service businesses with 5–50 employees that already use multiple software tools and are wasting 10+ hours a month on repetitive tasks.”[^2][^3]
3. Revenue model and 3-year projections
    - “I sell fixed-price projects, typically 5,000–8,000 each, deployed into the client’s own cloud so they own everything.”[^3]
    - “Year 1: I’m targeting 90,000 in revenue from about one to two projects per month, leading to about 80,749 net profit after all expenses and loan interest.”[^1]
    - “Year 2: 135,000 revenue; Year 3: 180,000 revenue, with profits growing as I reuse templates and referrals kick in.”[^1][^3]
4. Expenses and loan use
    - “My fixed costs are low: operating expenses in Year 1 are 8,729 total, including hardware, software, insurance, marketing, and travel.”[^1]
    - “The 6,500 loan is for a laptop, Oracle Cloud, insurance, LLC/legal setup, and initial marketing, exactly as outlined in my plan.”[^3]
    - “After that, the business operates on cash flow; I’m not planning to stack other credit on top while this loan is outstanding.”[^3]
5. Repayment story
    - “Even with just one project a month at my starting prices, I comfortably cover operating costs and loan payments.”[^3][^1]
    - “Net profit in Year 1 alone is over 80,000; that gives a lot of cushion to repay a 6,500 microloan on time.”[^1]
6. Risk and backup plan
    - “If client acquisition is slower than expected, I keep overhead low, push more free assessments, and I’m prepared to take part-time contract work to make sure payments are covered.”[^4][^3]
    - “I limit scope to projects within my current technical ability and use clear fixed-price scopes to reduce project risk.”[^3]

## Likely lender questions and short answers

Use short, calm sentences, then stop and let them ask follow-ups.

- “How did you come up with 90,000 in Year 1?”
“It’s based on 15–18 projects at 5,000–6,000 each, which matches the pricing and capacity in my plan: one to two projects per month.”[^1][^3]
- “What if you only hit half your revenue?”
“Even at half, the combination of low overhead and my ability to take side contract work covers the loan. The projections are conservative; I only need a few clients to cover expenses.”[^4][^3][^1]
- “Where will your clients come from?”
“Direct outreach to my local network, a free workflow assessment offer, LinkedIn content for Hawaii service businesses, and local business events.”[^3]
- “Do you plan to take on more debt?”
“No. I view this as a one-time ramp-up. After this loan, I intend to operate on business cash flow, not ongoing credit.”
- “What happens if tech changes or something breaks?”
“I’m using well-supported tools like self-hosted n8n on Oracle Cloud, and I include 90 days of support plus documentation so clients are not locked into me.”[^3]


## How to practice tonight

- Read each numbered section aloud until you can say it without looking.
- Especially lock in: 90,000 / 135,000 / 180,000, 8,729 operating expenses Year 1, 80,749 net profit Year 1, 6,500 loan amount.[^1]
- Do 3–4 run-throughs where you answer the likely questions out loud; keep each answer to 15–25 seconds so you sound **confident**, not rushed.[^2][^4]
